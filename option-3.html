Helios Robotics · Tuesday, 12 May 2026 · T-8 months from S-1 target

The morning the workpaper stopped being a document.

A short story in eight scenes about the day Anton Dam walked Naomi Wilkes through Helios's Q1 SOX testing, and what changed when every claim on the screen carried its own citation.

06:42 AM · Kitchen table · Berkeley, CA

Scene 1

Anton opens RC-047, alone, with a third coffee.

Eight months from the S-1. Anton has been at Helios for fourteen months — long enough to know where every account reconciliation lives, short enough that he still sometimes opens the wrong NetSuite saved search. The Deloitte walkthrough is at 9:00 AM. He is reviewing RC-047 one more time, the same way he has reviewed every Q1 control for the last six weeks: with a yellow legal pad next to his laptop, ready to write down anything he can't immediately defend.

He opens the workpaper. The threshold field reads $250,000. He hovers it without thinking.

PROVENANCE · RC-047 · threshold_amount_usd live read · 2026-05-12 06:42
$250,000 — sourced from NetSuite workflow rule WF-MJE-RR-047, captured 2026-01-09 18:02 PT by Pranav Iyer (Controller). Last modified 2026-01-09: threshold raised from $200k to $250k per Q1 2026 materiality memo M-2026-Q1-MJE. No overrides on file. rec_ns_wf_047_threshold

Last year he would have spent twenty minutes pulling up the NetSuite admin panel, the memo, the change-log, the materiality calc, and stitching the answer together by hand. He'd have written a defensive paragraph in the workpaper itself ("threshold reviewed quarterly against current-period materiality benchmark per memo M-2026-Q1-MJE") and crossed his fingers that no partner would ask which memo. This year he just hovered the number.

08:51 AM · Conference room A · Helios HQ · Fremont

Scene 2

Naomi Wilkes arrives nine minutes early.

Naomi Wilkes is the Deloitte engagement partner. She's been on the Helios audit for two years and on pre-IPO engagements for fifteen. She has seen seven different vendors' workpaper outputs in that time. Most of them look the same: a Word document with a tickmark legend at the bottom and a wet signature on page seven.

She sits down. Anton shares his screen.

"Walk me through how you tested manual journal entry approval this quarter, Anton."
09:02 AM · Same conference room

Scene 3

The first thing the partner asks for, the workpaper already answers.

This is the moment, every walkthrough, where the Controller has to alt-tab to a different application — the source system, the document vault, the memo folder — to defend the number Naomi just pointed at. Anton doesn't.

He just hovers.

PROVENANCE · RC-047 · population_count live read · 2026-05-12 09:02
218 manual entries — sourced from NetSuite saved search NS-Saved-Search-MJE-ALL-047, captured 2026-04-08 14:12 PT by Anton Dam. No manual edits since capture; sha256 verified at ingest. rec_ns_pop_047_q1

Naomi looks at it for a second longer than she needs to. Then she nods and moves on.

09:15 AM · Same conference room

Scene 4

"Show me the exceptions."

Anton scrolls to the exceptions section. There are two — both for Q1 — both already dispositioned. JE-2026-0098 (illegible initials) and JE-2026-0214 (a threshold-bypass override on a $248,900 deferred-revenue true-up).

Naomi hovers the override entry herself. She has been told about this UI, but it's the first time she's used it.

PROVENANCE · RC-047 · override · ovr_rc047_021 live read · 2026-05-12 09:15
"Period-end true-up to deferred-revenue waterfall; corresponds to ticket SF-2026-0419."
Entered by Anton Dam, 2026-02-26 15:14 PT. Reviewed by Pranav Iyer, 2026-02-27 08:50 PT. 1 override on file for this control this quarter. rec_optro_ovr_rc047_021

What just happened: Naomi asked the question every partner asks ("did anyone get around the threshold this quarter, and if so, why"), and the workpaper itself answered — with the rationale, the entry timestamp, the reviewer timestamp, and the ticket number it ties back to. She didn't need to ask anyone. She didn't need to wait for a follow-up. She just hovered.

For the first time in fifteen years she wasn't presenting to Deloitte — they were reading along with her.
09:48 AM · Same conference room

Scene 5

The chain view comes up next.

Naomi asks for the evidence chain. Anton clicks one tab. The graph shows the control at the center, the seven testing procedures as spokes, the seven artifacts orbiting their parent procedures, the three sign-offs as edges.

Naomi traces P7 — the threshold-review procedure — out to the memo at the artifact layer. She hovers the memo. The provenance shows it was uploaded by Pranav Iyer on 2026-01-09 with a sha256 hash. Naomi has seen vendors render this as a hyperlink to a SharePoint folder. She has not seen it rendered as a verified link with a hash and a capture timestamp.

She makes a note in her own work paper. The note says: "defensibility evidence rendered live against source-of-record — no manual reconciliation required at audit."

10:08 AM · Peer benchmark tab

Scene 6

The remediation surfaces inside the walkthrough.

This is the moment Anton has been worrying about for two weeks.

He clicks the peer-benchmark tab. The screen shows three metrics for RC-047 compared to 47 anonymized pre-IPO peers in the technology vertical. The numbers are not flattering.

PEER BENCHMARK · RC-047 · reviewer_signoff_layers live read · cohort N=47 · S-1 window Jan '24 – Apr '25
Helios at the 22nd percentile. Peer median is 2.0 sign-off layers per control; Helios averages 1.4. Single sign-off on 60% of controls, dual on 40%. Largest single gap to peer median across all defensibility metrics.

Last year this conversation would have happened in a finding letter, six weeks after the walkthrough, written by an audit senior who had to track down the data manually across nine controls and three quarters. Anton would have spent his Thanksgiving weekend pulling together a remediation plan that he had not had time to think about properly.

This year the gap surfaces on the screen, mid-walkthrough, while everyone with the authority to remediate it is in the room. By 11:00 AM Pranav has texted Tara (the CAE) a one-line proposal: "Mandate two-layer sign-off on all Big-4-visible controls before the management assertion package. ETA 6 weeks." Tara replies with a thumbs-up emoji from her car. Naomi files the remediation as in-flight rather than open.

11:14 AM · Same conference room · 14 minutes after the walkthrough ends

Scene 7

Print to PDF.

Naomi asks for a clean copy of the workpaper to take back to her team. Anton clicks Print to PDF. The output is a paper-style document with no hover affordances, a tickmark legend at the bottom, the Optro Provenance v1 signature in the footer, and a list of every optro_record_id the document references. It is twelve pages.

It looks, on paper, like a Big-4-style workpaper has always looked. But every claim in it can be re-verified, on demand, against the live system. The PDF is a snapshot — but the snapshot is of a chain, not a document.

02:30 PM · Anton's desk · same day

Scene 8

The note Naomi wrote in her own work paper.

Naomi sent her engagement file back to Boston that afternoon. The note in her work paper read:

"Helios's Q1 controls testing was reviewed using Optro Provenance v1. Defensibility evidence is rendered live against the underlying source-of-record systems at the point of review. Reduces substantive testing requirement on selected revenue-cycle controls. Recommend the rest of the engagement team review the format before next quarter."

That note — generated mid-walkthrough by a partner who had spent fifteen years reading workpapers built the old way — is what Optro is selling. The system of record is the moat. Provenance is what the moat looks like once it is rendered.
